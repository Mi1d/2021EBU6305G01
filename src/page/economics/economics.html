Introduction

    The Silk Road refers to the ancient commercial trade route starting from ancient China and connecting Asia, Africa and Europe.
    In terms of transportation mode, it is divided into land Silk Road and Maritime Silk Road. The land Silk Road began in Chang'an, the capital of the Western Han Dynasty (extending to Luoyang in the Eastern Han Dynasty).
    The silk road is a major road for economic, political and cultural exchanges between the East and the West.

The opening of the silk road effectively promoted the economic and cultural exchanges between the East and the West and played a positive role in promoting the prosperity of the Han Dynasty
This silk road is still an important channel for exchanges between China and the West. At the moment of industrialization, it has completed its mission It has been replaced by the 10900 km long new Eurasian land bridge from Lianyungang in the east to Rotterdam in the West However, it still has new exploitable value.

Western Han Dynasty

In the pre Qin period, a channel connecting the East and the west of China already existed. The official transmission of silk to the West began in the Western Han Dynasty, and the real formation of the silk road began with Zhang Qian's air drilling in the Western Han Dynasty.
During this period, the source, destination and route of silk communication were very clear. There was a history to rely on and a record to check. The number of silk communication was also very large. The East and the West carried out silk trade in a planned or even organized way. Therefore, the silk road was really opened up during the period of Emperor Wu of the Western Han Dynasty.
It can be traced back to the period of Emperor Wu of the Western Han Dynasty. At that time, the silk road was actually born for political purposes, not the product of commercial development to a certain stage.

Tang -

In the Tang Dynasty, the silk road basically continued the route of the previous generation and crossed the Eurasian continent. The Silk Road of the Tang Dynasty was developed, which dredged some sections interrupted by war since the northern and Southern Dynasties, and opened a section of Tianshan North Road. The Silk Road of the Tang Dynasty started from Chang'an (now Xi'an), passed through Gansu Hexi Corridor (there are parallel lines in Qinghai and Ningxia) to Xinjiang, crossed Tianshan Mountain North and south to Central Asia, one way to the Middle East and the other to Europe. After crossing Tianshan Mountain, there is another route to India.

In the Tang Dynasty, there was a climax of economic and cultural exchanges between the East and the west, and the silk road was also very prosperous.
The content and characteristics of the Silk Road in the Tang Dynasty were the prosperity and alternation of land and sea routes. As the climax of the land Silk Road in the early Tang Dynasty, it is only the "peak" of the development of the land Silk Road since the Han Dynasty,
Since then, the land silk road has lost its development advantages (compared with the maritime Silk Road, of course);
After the Mid Tang Dynasty, the maritime silk road was also in an important turning period in its development history. Therefore, to understand the development of the maritime Silk Road, it is necessary to study the changes and alternations of the land and Sea Silk Road in the Tang Dynasty.
The world-famous silk road is not only a friendly bridge between the East and the west, but also an important link connecting all ethnic groups in China.
Therefore, from the date of the formation of the Silk Road, the general trend of its development is not only to the East and West, but also to the north and south.
Since the Han Dynasty, the land silk road has gradually formed three basic trunk lines of East-West communication: North, middle and south along the north and south of Tianshan Mountain; At the same time, the frequent activities of various nationalities in the northern and southern frontier fortresses laid the foundation for the expansion of the Silk Road to the north and South in the Tang Dynasty.
In the fourth year of Zhenguan (A.D. 630), Emperor Taizong Li Shimin led the army to defeat the aristocratic regime of the eastern Turks, strengthened friendly ties with the Western Turks, and then swept away the separatist forces such as Gaochang, Yanqi and kuci.
In the 14th year of Zhenguan (A.D. 640), the Tang Dynasty established Anxi Dadu protectorate in the western regions, which ruled all subordinate dududufu and prefectures, further strengthened the military and administrative management of the western frontier and ensured the prosperity and smoothness of the silk road.


During the Zhenguan period of the Tang Dynasty, Chinese monks visited the eastern route of India and recorded in detail the specific route from Hexi, Qinghai and Tibet to Nepal.
The opening of the above branch line not only shows the prosperity and smoothness of the road, but also reflects the great expansion of the Silk Road to the south.
Another obvious reflection of the development peak of the Silk Road in the early Tang Dynasty is the importance and prosperity of the North Road of the Silk Road and the emergence of some emerging cities and trade centers along the North Road.

Song Dynasty -

The actual territory of the Northern Song Dynasty was greatly reduced, and the government failed to control the Hexi corridor. In the Southern Song Dynasty, it was even less able to set foot in the northwest. The decline of the silk road was increasingly obvious, while the rise of the maritime silk road gradually showed signs of replacing the land silk road.
According to historical records, European merchants, merchants from west and Central Asia and Chinese Semu merchants were engaged in caravan trafficking trade on the long East-West land road at that time.
European, central and West Asian merchants generally bring large quantities of gold and silver, jewelry, medicine, exotic birds and animals, spices, bamboo cloth and other commodities to China or sell them along the way,
What they buy are mainly Chinese satins, embroidered colors, gold brocade, silk, tea, porcelain, medicinal materials and other commodities.

During the Yuan Dynasty, the countries of the "Silk Road Economic Belt" mainly refer to the four major khanates of Mongolia: qincha khanate, wokuotai khanate, Chagatai Khanate and Yili Khanate. At this time, the Weiwuer area has been attached to the Yuan Dynasty, not included.
Qincha khanate, also known as golden Khanate or shuchi wuruth, developed from the fief of shuchi. It includes the eastern part of qincha grassland to the east of zayaheishui (now Ural River), the northern part of huarazimu in the lower reaches of Amu Darya River and the lower reaches of huchan river flowing into the Aral Sea.
Chagatai Khanate developed from the fief of Chagatai, reaching Turpan in the East, the Amu Darya River in the west, huarazima and tarbahatai mountains in the north and India in the south.
The transportation between the Yuan Dynasty and central and Western Asia basically depended on the post roads built by the northwest khanates and the Yuan Dynasty. The merchant caravans of various countries and the envoys and transportation teams of the emperors and kings of the Yuan Dynasty could only shuttle between the regions by post roads.
The post road is full of risks, and there are many robbers who rob property. In addition to providing necessary food and accommodation for merchants, the post station along the way can also provide armed protection, which is superior to other routes.
Therefore, clarifying the transportation routes between the northwest countries and the Yuan Dynasty will basically clarify the gold and silver circulation routes between the Yuan Dynasty and the northwest countries.

（1） North Road Base
According to the traffic route map between China and foreign countries in the Yuan Dynasty, from Dadu to the west, through Xuande and Datong, continue to the west, pass Fengzhou and Ningxia, reach Ganzhou (now Zhangye, Gansu), and then from hening to the West or southwest to besi Bali (now the northeast of Qitai, Xinjiang), hamili (now Hami, Xinjiang) and Ali respectively In Mali (now jiuhuocheng, Xinjiang) and other places, the above roads connect the Silk Road since the Han and Tang Dynasties. This section of the road is known as the "North Road" between China and West Asia. It was renovated during Genghis Khan's western expedition.

（2） South Road
Although the "North Road" has been repaired, the road is still very difficult. So after Taizong wokuotai succeeded to the throne, he ordered Zhu you to "set up a post from Dunhuang to Yuguan and connect with the western regions", and opened up the "South Road". Until Ali Buge and Haidu rebelled, the "South Road" has always been one of the important business routes of Han and Northwest Khanate in the Mongolian and Yuan Dynasties. "South Road" was initially based on the road of "connecting the western regions" according to Zhu you.

（3） Other roads
AfterthenorthwestKhanate andtheYuan Dynasty,theqincha Khanate was attached to the suzerain state of the Yuan Dynasty, and the original road block no longer exists. At that time European businessmen were able to reach Dadu through Yulong Jiechi of qincha Khanate. In the first half of the 14th century, the commerce of Tana was controlled by the merchants of Genoa and Venice. Many European businessmen set out from here to engage in trade activities in China. 明 -

The maritime Silk Road of the Ming Dynasty had a turning point: first, from prosperity to decline, and second, the new opening of the American silk road.
The Ming Dynasty was a critical period for the reversal of Sino foreign relations. Zheng He's voyages to the West in the early Ming Dynasty created a feat of foreign economic exchanges at sea. However, after the middle of the Ming Dynasty, resisting foreign aggression and combating folk maritime smuggling became a new feature of foreign political and economic relations.
During this period, the implementation of the "sea ban" and closed door policy seriously hindered the non-governmental economic and cultural exchanges between China and foreign countries, and there were new changes in currency circulation.
The monetary and cultural characteristics of Zheng He's seven trips to the West in the early Ming Dynasty the economic exchanges along the maritime Silk Road in the early Ming Dynasty were still the main destinations along the coasts of Asia and Africa.


（1） China traded silk for gold and silver from Arab merchants
One of Zheng He's missions was to look for gold in East Africa, but the origin of gold was inland in West Africa. At that time, only Arabs could obtain gold through black chiefs. Arab businessmen brought gold to China's ports to do business with the Chinese authorities, so the gold and silver flowed into the official Treasury.

（2） The government monopolizes trade and implements a strict foreign exchange control policy.
In order to obtain much-needed gold and silver, monopolize official trade and prohibit non-governmental foreign economic exchanges at sea, another task of Zheng He was to destroy China's non-governmental overseas trade base by force



After the middle and late Ming Dynasty, the monetary culture of the new maritime Silk Road "discovered" America from Columbus, Spain, and great changes have taken place in international trade. Because America was rich in gold and silver, 80% of the world's silver came from America at that time. Spain occupied a large area of American land and set up factories to mint silver coins in the silver rich American colonies,
These silver coins crossed the Pacific Ocean through Acapulco port in Mexico, and arrived in Fujian and Guangdong along the southeast coast of China after transit through Luzon (i.e. the Philippines). After trading, they also transported Chinese silk and porcelain to America. This maritime trade route is known as the "medieval maritime American Silk Road". At this time, the status of Arab businessmen in international trade declined rapidly,
Because West African gold is no longer needed for foreign trade, Europeans can buy Chinese products with American silver. The opening of new routes has extended China's Maritime Silk Road to the Americas, which is epoch-making. After the 12th year of Zhengde of Ming Dynasty (1517), the direct trade between Europe, America and China has gradually developed from scratch to a considerable scale. In the middle and late Ming Dynasty, the starting point of the trade route between China and the Americas was Fujian Yuegang (now Longhai Haicheng)


（1） Silver currency became the main currency and the silver standard monetary system was established. After the establishment of the Ming Dynasty, the paper money - Daming treasure note was used throughout the country, and the use of gold and silver as circulation means was prohibited. But before long, the value of paper money became more and more iron. By the mid-15th century, it was forced to allow silver as a means of circulation and waste paper money was not used. Since then, silver has become China's standard currency and copper money as a secondary currency. At that time, 90% of the market traded in silver and less than 10% in copper. With the huge amount of overseas silver flowing into China, the pace of China's monetary economy has been accelerated, and China's silver standard monetary system has been gradually established.

Enlightenment of Maritime Silk currency culture

（1） It is inevitable to use value symbols instead of full value gold and silver coins. Gold and silver do not act as a trading medium, but become valuable commodities representing wealth.
The role of gold and silver is much smaller now than in ancient times, because the real paper money system was determined and prevailed. Whether it is the RMB, the US dollar, the euro or the currencies of other countries, paper money (of course, a small number of coins) is used without exception and is no longer linked to gold and silver.
Paper money is just a value symbol. It has no value in itself, but only represents value and wealth. However, it brings great convenience to commodity exchange and currency exchange, promotes the rapid development of trade and symbolizes the progress of the times.


（2） Sufficient value of gold and silver can preserve its own value. In order to comply with the law of money circulation, paper money without value itself must also maintain the relative stability of its representative value. The "law of money circulation" requires that the circulation of money should be consistent with the actual demand, that is, the value represented by money is equal to the value of goods and services. Gold and silver coins themselves are a special commodity with value and use value, which can automatically preserve the value and will not lead to a sharp rise, depreciation and fluctuation of the currency. But paper money doesn't work. It has no value in itself. Its face value represents a certain amount of commodity value, which is easy to fluctuate violently under the influence of circulation.
（3） It is necessary and inevitable to maintain the balance of international monetary exchange. When the colonial countries of Western Europe began to expand overseas in the 16th century, China was still an advanced world power that did not lag behind others.But China's backwardness began at this time. In the era when countries vigorously developed foreign trade by using sea channels, China, which was originally highly developed, was increasingly closed. The Ming Dynasty implemented the conservative policy of banning private overseas trade, resulting in the loss of the opportunity to keep up with the trend of development. If the Ming Dynasty continued to implement the active overseas trade policy of Yongle and Xuande at that time, China's overseas trade would flourish with China's navigation and national economic strength at that time.
