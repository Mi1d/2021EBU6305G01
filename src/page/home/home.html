// About The Silk Road

Introduction to the Silk Road

The Silk Road generally refers to the Land Silk Road, and broadly speaking, it is divided into the Land Silk Road and the Maritime Silk Road.
The overland Silk Road originated from the overland passage opened up by Emperor Wudi of the Western Han Dynasty when Zhang Qian sent an envoy to the Western Regions, starting from the capital Chang'an (now Xi'an), passing through Gansu and Xinjiang, to Central Asia and West Asia, and connecting the Mediterranean countries. The starting point of the Silk Road in the Eastern Han Dynasty was Luoyang. Its original function was to transport silk produced in ancient China, and it became a comprehensive trade route during the Ming Dynasty. In 1877, the German geogeographer Richthofen, in his book "China", described "from 114 BC to 127 AD, the silk trade between China and Central Asia, China and India as a medium. A traffic road in the Western Regions" was named "Silk Road", this term was quickly accepted by the academic circles and the public, and was officially used.

Overview of the Silk Road

The traditional Silk Road starts from Chang'an, the ancient capital of China, and reaches the Mediterranean Sea through Central Asian countries, Afghanistan, Iran, Iraq, Syria, etc., and ends at Rome, with a total length of 6,440 kilometers.
This road is considered to be the intersection of ancient eastern and western civilizations linking the Eurasian continent, and silk is the most representative cargo. For thousands of years, nomads or tribes, merchants, cultists, diplomats, soldiers and academic explorers traveled along the Silk Road.
With the development of the times, the Silk Road has become a collective name for all political, economic and cultural exchanges between ancient China and the West. There is the "Silk Road in the Northwest", the official passage to the Western Regions opened by Zhang Qian of the Western Han Dynasty; there is the "Silk Road in the Prairie", which goes north to the Mongolian Plateau, and then goes westward to the northern foot of the Tianshan Mountains to enter Central Asia; In the Ming Dynasty, there were also the "Maritime Silk Road" for maritime trade starting from Guangzhou, Quanzhou, Hangzhou, Yangzhou and other coastal cities, from the South China Sea to the Arabian Sea, and even as far as the east coast of Africa.

Western Han Dynasty

The official western transmission of silk began in the Western Han Dynasty and the Western Regions, and the real formation of the Silk Road began in the Western Han Dynasty when Zhang Qian hollowed it out.
During this period, the source, destination, and route of transmission of silk were all very clear. There was historical evidence and evidence to check. The amount of transmission was also very large.
The East and the West were planning and even organized silk trade , so the Silk Road really opened up in the period of Emperor Wu of the Western Han Dynasty.

Eastern Han Dynasty

In the sixteenth year of Yongping (73 years), Ban Chao followed the general Dou Gu to attack the northern Xiongnu and was ordered to go to the Western Regions. He led 36 officials and officials to Shanshan first, and with his determination to "don't enter the tiger's den, how can we get tiger sons", Shanshan was shocked. Later, he persuaded Khotan to join the central government.
Ban Chao re-opened the Silk Road, which had been isolated for 58 years, and helped the countries in the Western Regions to get rid of the control of the Xiongnu. He was appointed as the Protector of the Western Regions by the Eastern Han Dynasty. Ban Chao operated in the Western Regions for 30 years, strengthening the connection between the Western Regions and the interior.

Wei and Jin Dynasties

During the Wei, Jin, Southern and Northern Dynasties, the Silk Road continued to develop, mainly including the Northwest Silk Road (also known as the Oasis Silk Road or the Desert Silk Road), the Southwest Silk Road and the Maritime Silk Road.
It has three characteristics: the transition from the Han Dynasty to the Sui and Tang Dynasties, the further development of the Maritime Silk Road, and the frequent exchanges between the North and South regimes and the Western Regions at the same time.

Sui Dynasty

In the ninth year of Kaihuang in the Sui Dynasty (589), the Sui Dynasty ended the north-south split, and the emerging Turkic people occupied the vast area between the Western Regions and the Caspian Sea. Today, Tuyuhun in Qinghai also invaded the Hexi Corridor. China and the Western Regions, the official and non-governmental exchanges in the West were affected. Lots of obstacles.
However, the relationship between the Sui Dynasty and the peoples of the countries along the Silk Road was still getting closer and closer. The merchants in the Western Regions often reached Zhangye Mutual Market. Emperor Yang once sent Pei Ju to take charge of this work. Pei Ju lured them to the mainland with generous gifts, so that their exchanges were successive.

Tang Dynasty

The prosperous period of the Silk Road exchanges was the powerful Tang Dynasty established after the Sui Dynasty.
The smooth and prosperous Silk Road in the Tang Dynasty further promoted the ideological and cultural exchanges between the East and the West, and had a lot of positive and far-reaching influence on the mutual social and national ideological development in the future. This kind of ideological and cultural exchange is closely related to religion. related.

Song and Yuan Dynasties

The actual territory of the Northern Song Dynasty was greatly reduced, the government failed to control the Hexi Corridor, and in the Southern Song Dynasty, it was even less able to set foot in the northwest region.
The purpose of exchanges on the Silk Road in the Yuan Dynasty has changed significantly. Most of them are religious and cultural exchanges as the mission, instead of being dominated by businessmen, which reflects the decline of the Silk Road from the side.

Ming Dynasty

A "Silk Road Landscape Map" drawn in the Jiajing year in the middle of the Ming Dynasty clearly marked the names of more than 200 locations on the "Silk Road". It was completed in the form of landscape painting and reproduced with superb painting skills. The magnificent momentum of the cities and mountains along the way.
"Silk Road Landscape Map" is extremely elegant and appropriate, and it is likely to be the original name of the Ming Dynasty. The picture on the map starts from Jiayuguan and takes the westbound road of Kuyucheng, which is the route of the Silk Road in the Ming Dynasty.

Qing Dynasty

After the early Qing Dynasty, the government adopted a policy of seclusion and isolation. At the same time, shipbuilding technology and navigation technology continued to develop, and maritime traffic took its place, making the Silk Road trade decline in an all-round way.

Silk Road Rebirth

On September 7, 2013, Chinese President Xi Jinping delivered an important speech entitled "Promoting People's Friendship and Creating a Better Future" at Nazarbayev University in Kazakhstan. The friendly cooperation policy advocates the use of innovative cooperation models to jointly build the "Silk Road Economic Belt" as a major cause that benefits the people of all countries along the way.

Southern Silk Road

Around the 4th century BC, when the Central Plains were divided, a Silk Road was opened between Shudi (now the western Sichuan Plain) and the body.
Until Zhang Qian went to the Western Regions, he discovered in Daxia that Shu cloth and Qiong bamboo sticks were trafficked from body drugs. After he reported to Emperor Wu of Han, Emperor Wu of Han sent Zhang Qian to get through the "Shu - Emperor Wu of the Han Dynasty" in the first year of Yuanshou (122 BC). Poison Road".
The Southern Silk Road consists of three roads, namely Lingguan Road, Wuchi Road and Yongchang Road. The Southern Silk Road lasted for more than 2,000 years. Especially during the Anti-Japanese War, the sea passage from the rear was cut off. The Yunnan-Burma Highway and the China-India Highway opened along the Southwest Road of the Silk Road were unprecedentedly busy and became a lifeline to support the rear.

// Southern & Northern Silk Road

Northern Silk Road

The Desert Oasis Silk Road is the main road of the Northern Silk Road. The eastern section from Chang'an to Dunhuang is relatively stable compared to the central and western section, but the west of Chang'an is divided into 3 lines.
Among them, the North Road from Jingchuan, Guyuan, Jingyuan to Wuwei has the shortest route, but there is a shortage of water along the way and the supply is not easy. The middle route goes from Jingchuan to Pingliang, Huining, Lanzhou and Wuwei. The distance and supply are moderate. The South Road runs from Fengxiang, Tianshui, Longxi, Linxia, Ledu, Xining to Zhangye, but the journey is long.
The three routes of the South Road, the Middle Road and the North Road are not a "road", but an unmarked road network that crosses mountains and deserts, and silk is only one kind of goods.
The choice of each route should consider the safety and convenience of crossing Liupan Mountain and crossing the Yellow River. The three lines all start from Chang'an or Luoyang, join in Wuwei and Zhangye, and then follow the Hexi Corridor to Dunhuang.
